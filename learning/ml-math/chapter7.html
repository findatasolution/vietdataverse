1. Number Systems

1.1 Binary Numbers (Base-2)

Binary uses only digits $\{0, 1\}$ with place values as powers of 2.

Binary to Decimal Conversion:

$$(b_n b_{n-1} \ldots b_1 b_0)_2 = b_n \cdot 2^n + b_{n-1} \cdot 2^{n-1} + \cdots + b_1 \cdot 2 + b_0$$
Example 1.1: Binary to Decimal

Convert $(1101)_2$ to decimal:

$$ \begin{align} (1101)_2 &= 1 \cdot 2^3 + 1 \cdot 2^2 + 0 \cdot 2^1 + 1 \cdot 2^0 \\ &= 8 + 4 + 0 + 1 \\ &= 13_{10} \end{align} $$
Example 1.2: Decimal to Binary

Convert $25_{10}$ to binary using repeated division by 2:

$$ \begin{align} 25 \div 2 &= 12 \text{ remainder } \mathbf{1} \\ 12 \div 2 &= 6 \text{ remainder } \mathbf{0} \\ 6 \div 2 &= 3 \text{ remainder } \mathbf{0} \\ 3 \div 2 &= 1 \text{ remainder } \mathbf{1} \\ 1 \div 2 &= 0 \text{ remainder } \mathbf{1} \end{align} $$

Reading remainders from bottom to top: $(11001)_2$

1.2 Hexadecimal Numbers (Base-16)

Hexadecimal uses digits $\{0, 1, 2, \ldots, 9, A, B, C, D, E, F\}$ where $A=10, B=11, \ldots, F=15$.

Example 1.3: Hexadecimal Conversions

Hex to Decimal: $(2F)_{16}$ to decimal

$$(2F)_{16} = 2 \cdot 16^1 + 15 \cdot 16^0 = 32 + 15 = 47_{10}$$

Binary to Hex: $(11010110)_2$ to hex

Group into sets of 4 bits: $(1101)_2 = D_{16}$ and $(0110)_2 = 6_{16}$

Result: $(D6)_{16}$

1.3 Octal Numbers (Base-8)

Octal uses digits $\{0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7\}$.

Example 1.4: Octal Conversion

Convert $(765)_8$ to decimal:

$$(765)_8 = 7 \cdot 8^2 + 6 \cdot 8^1 + 5 \cdot 8^0 = 448 + 48 + 5 = 501_{10}$$
ML Application: Number systems in computing:
  • Binary: Fundamental representation in computers, binary classification
  • Hexadecimal: Memory addresses, color codes, compact notation
  • Floating-point: IEEE 754 standard for real numbers in ML

2. Divisibility & Prime Numbers

2.1 Divisibility

We say $a$ divides $b$ (written $a \mid b$) if there exists an integer $k$ such that $b = ka$.

Properties:

  • If $a \mid b$ and $b \mid c$, then $a \mid c$ (transitive)
  • If $a \mid b$ and $a \mid c$, then $a \mid (b + c)$ and $a \mid (b - c)$
  • If $a \mid b$ and $b \neq 0$, then $|a| \leq |b|$

2.2 Prime Numbers

A prime number is an integer $p > 1$ that has exactly two divisors: $1$ and $p$.

Example 2.1: Prime Factorization

Find the prime factorization of $360$:

$$ \begin{align} 360 &= 2 \cdot 180 \\ &= 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 90 \\ &= 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 45 \\ &= 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 3 \cdot 15 \\ &= 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 3 \cdot 3 \cdot 5 \\ &= 2^3 \cdot 3^2 \cdot 5 \end{align} $$

2.3 GCD (Greatest Common Divisor)

The GCD of $a$ and $b$, written $\gcd(a, b)$, is the largest integer that divides both $a$ and $b$.

Example 2.2: GCD by Prime Factorization

Find $\gcd(48, 180)$:

$$ \begin{align} 48 &= 2^4 \cdot 3 \\ 180 &= 2^2 \cdot 3^2 \cdot 5 \end{align} $$ $$\gcd(48, 180) = 2^{\min(4,2)} \cdot 3^{\min(1,2)} = 2^2 \cdot 3 = 12$$

2.4 Euclidean Algorithm

An efficient method to compute GCD based on the principle: $\gcd(a, b) = \gcd(b, a \bmod b)$

Algorithm:

  1. If $b = 0$, return $a$
  2. Otherwise, compute $r = a \bmod b$
  3. Replace $(a, b)$ with $(b, r)$ and repeat
Example 2.3: Euclidean Algorithm

Find $\gcd(252, 105)$:

$$ \begin{align} \gcd(252, 105) &= \gcd(105, 252 \bmod 105) = \gcd(105, 42) \\ &= \gcd(42, 105 \bmod 42) = \gcd(42, 21) \\ &= \gcd(21, 42 \bmod 21) = \gcd(21, 0) \\ &= 21 \end{align} $$

3. Modular Arithmetic

3.1 Congruence

We say $a$ is congruent to $b$ modulo $n$ (written $a \equiv b \pmod{n}$) if $n \mid (a - b)$.

Properties of Modular Arithmetic:

  • $(a + b) \bmod n = ((a \bmod n) + (b \bmod n)) \bmod n$
  • $(a \cdot b) \bmod n = ((a \bmod n) \cdot (b \bmod n)) \bmod n$
  • If $a \equiv b \pmod{n}$, then $a^k \equiv b^k \pmod{n}$
Example 3.1: Modular Arithmetic

Compute $17 \cdot 23 \pmod{7}$:

$$ \begin{align} 17 \bmod 7 &= 3 \\ 23 \bmod 7 &= 2 \\ 17 \cdot 23 \pmod{7} &= (3 \cdot 2) \bmod 7 = 6 \end{align} $$

3.2 Modular Exponentiation

Computing $a^n \bmod m$ efficiently using repeated squaring.

Example 3.2: Modular Exponentiation

Compute $5^{11} \bmod 7$:

$$ \begin{align} 5^1 \bmod 7 &= 5 \\ 5^2 \bmod 7 &= 25 \bmod 7 = 4 \\ 5^4 \bmod 7 &= 4^2 \bmod 7 = 16 \bmod 7 = 2 \\ 5^8 \bmod 7 &= 2^2 \bmod 7 = 4 \end{align} $$ $$5^{11} = 5^8 \cdot 5^2 \cdot 5^1$$ $$5^{11} \bmod 7 = (4 \cdot 4 \cdot 5) \bmod 7 = 80 \bmod 7 = 3$$

4. RSA Cryptography

4.1 Public-Key Cryptography

RSA is a public-key cryptosystem based on the difficulty of factoring large numbers.

Key Generation:

  1. Choose two large prime numbers $p$ and $q$
  2. Compute $n = p \cdot q$
  3. Compute $\phi(n) = (p-1)(q-1)$ (Euler's totient function)
  4. Choose $e$ such that $\gcd(e, \phi(n)) = 1$ (public exponent)
  5. Compute $d$ such that $e \cdot d \equiv 1 \pmod{\phi(n)}$ (private exponent)
  6. Public key: $(n, e)$
  7. Private key: $(n, d)$

Encryption & Decryption:

  • Encryption: $c = m^e \bmod n$ (where $m$ is message, $c$ is ciphertext)
  • Decryption: $m = c^d \bmod n$
Example 4.1: Simple RSA

Key Generation:

$$ \begin{align} p &= 3, \quad q = 11 \\ n &= p \cdot q = 33 \\ \phi(n) &= (p-1)(q-1) = 2 \cdot 10 = 20 \\ e &= 3 \quad (\gcd(3, 20) = 1) \\ d &= 7 \quad (3 \cdot 7 = 21 \equiv 1 \pmod{20}) \end{align} $$

Public key: $(33, 3)$, Private key: $(33, 7)$

Encrypt message $m = 5$:

$$c = 5^3 \bmod 33 = 125 \bmod 33 = 26$$

Decrypt ciphertext $c = 26$:

$$m = 26^7 \bmod 33$$ $$26^2 = 676 \equiv 16 \pmod{33}$$ $$26^4 \equiv 16^2 = 256 \equiv 25 \pmod{33}$$ $$26^7 = 26^4 \cdot 26^2 \cdot 26 \equiv 25 \cdot 16 \cdot 26 \pmod{33}$$ $$= 10400 \equiv 5 \pmod{33}$$

Recovered message: $m = 5$ ✓

ML Application: Cryptography in ML:
  • Secure computation: Encrypted machine learning (homomorphic encryption)
  • Privacy-preserving ML: Federated learning, differential privacy
  • Blockchain: Cryptographic hashing in distributed ML systems

5. Practice Problems

Practice 1: Number System Conversions

a) Convert $(101101)_2$ to decimal

Solution:

$$ (101101)_2 = 1 \cdot 2^5 + 0 \cdot 2^4 + 1 \cdot 2^3 + 1 \cdot 2^2 + 0 \cdot 2^1 + 1 \cdot 2^0 $$ $$= 32 + 0 + 8 + 4 + 0 + 1 = 45_{10}$$

b) Convert $(3A)_{16}$ to decimal

Solution:

$$(3A)_{16} = 3 \cdot 16^1 + 10 \cdot 16^0 = 48 + 10 = 58_{10}$$

Practice 2: GCD using Euclidean Algorithm

Find $\gcd(144, 60)$ using the Euclidean algorithm

Solution:

$$ \begin{align} \gcd(144, 60) &= \gcd(60, 144 \bmod 60) = \gcd(60, 24) \\ &= \gcd(24, 60 \bmod 24) = \gcd(24, 12) \\ &= \gcd(12, 24 \bmod 12) = \gcd(12, 0) \\ &= 12 \end{align} $$

Practice 3: Modular Arithmetic

a) Compute $25 \cdot 17 \pmod{9}$

Solution:

$$ 25 \bmod 9 = 7, \quad 17 \bmod 9 = 8 $$ $$25 \cdot 17 \pmod{9} = (7 \cdot 8) \bmod 9 = 56 \bmod 9 = 2$$

b) Compute $3^{10} \bmod 7$

Solution:

$$ \begin{align} 3^1 \bmod 7 &= 3 \\ 3^2 \bmod 7 &= 9 \bmod 7 = 2 \\ 3^4 \bmod 7 &= 2^2 \bmod 7 = 4 \\ 3^8 \bmod 7 &= 4^2 \bmod 7 = 16 \bmod 7 = 2 \end{align} $$ $$3^{10} = 3^8 \cdot 3^2$$ $$3^{10} \bmod 7 = (2 \cdot 2) \bmod 7 = 4$$
